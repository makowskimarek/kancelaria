The proposed provisions of the Salary Transparency Bill provide for consultation between employers and workplace trade unions. But what if a large employer does not have such organisations?

The draft Salary Transparency Act, published in early May, does not impose a detailed procedure for selecting employee representatives. It states that, in the absence of a trade union organisation at the workplace, a joint assessment of pay is to be carried out in consultation with employee representatives selected in accordance with the procedure adopted by the employer in question. This means that the employer should ensure a transparent, documented and verifiable method of selecting them.

In practice, I would not recommend simply having the employer appoint the representatives. Such a model could be challenged as a mere formality, particularly if the representatives are to participate in consultations regarding the pay gap, the joint pay assessment and remedial measures. A safer solution is to hold elections among employees, even if the draft bill does not technically describe every stage of such a procedure.

## Procedure for selecting representatives

The employer should therefore adopt a brief election procedure, e.g. in the form of an order, regulations or an internal notice. The procedure should specify at least: who may stand as a candidate, who is entitled to vote, how many representatives will be elected, how candidates are nominated, how voting takes place, how the result is determined, the duration of the mandate, and the matters on which representatives will represent employees.

The principle should be to ensure that all employees have a genuine opportunity to participate in the elections. This also applies to employees who are absent from the workplace, working remotely, in a hybrid or mobile capacity, or employed in different locations. Depending on the organisation, elections may be held traditionally, electronically or via a mixed method. However, it is crucial that the employer can subsequently demonstrate that information about the elections was effectively communicated to the workforce and that the process itself did not favour specific individuals.

It is good practice to allow employees themselves to nominate candidates, rather than just management or the HR department. A candidate should give their consent to stand for election. It is also worth setting a minimum deadline for the submission of nominations and a deadline for employees to familiarise themselves with the list of candidates before voting.

The voting process itself should be as simple as possible, but thoroughly documented. The employer should draw up a record of the election, including the date of the vote, the number of eligible employees, the number of votes cast, the number of votes for each candidate, and a list of those elected. In the case of electronic voting, it is worth ensuring that the results can be verified and that the confidentiality of votes is protected.

## Number of representatives

It is also important to determine the number of representatives. The draft does not specify this, so a solution appropriate to the size and structure of the company should be adopted. In a small company, one representative may be sufficient, but in a larger organisation it will be safer to elect several people, especially if the company comprises different departments, locations or groups of employees. This helps to avoid the criticism that consultations were conducted with a person who, in practice, did not represent the diverse workforce.

The representatives’ mandate should be clearly defined. It may be specified that they are authorised to participate in consultations regarding obligations arising from regulations on pay equality and transparency, in particular joint pay assessments, pay gap analyses and consultations on remedial measures. It is also worth stipulating that the performance of this function does not give representatives the right to access individual pay data to a greater extent than is necessary and in accordance with data protection regulations.

## Protection of personal data

Data protection must also be borne in mind. Employee representatives may come into contact with information that is sensitive from the organisation’s perspective, including data on pay, categories of employees and the pay gap. The employer should therefore ensure that appropriate authorisations and confidentiality instructions are in place, and that consultation materials are prepared in such a way as to avoid disclosing the personal data of specific employees unless necessary.

In practice, a minimum, secure operating model might look as follows: the employer announces the elections, accepts candidate nominations, conducts a vote among employees, draws up the minutes, informs the workforce of the result and retains the election records. Only representatives selected in this way should participate in consultations regarding the joint pay review. This approach better fulfils the objective of the proposed legislation than the arbitrary appointment of representatives by the employer.

For employers without trade unions, the election of employee representatives will therefore be one of the first practical preparatory steps. It is advisable to carry this out before the obligation to conduct a joint pay review arises, rather than only at the point of a dispute, an inspection or the need for urgent reporting. A properly documented selection of representatives may be of significant evidential value if the employer is required to demonstrate that consultations were actually carried out, rather than merely going through the motions.
