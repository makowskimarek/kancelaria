## What does the new draft legislation mean for employers and employees?

In recent days, a draft bill on strengthening the application of the right to equal pay for men and women for work of equal value or work of equal worth has been under consideration on the website of the Government Legislation Centre. The draft, designated UC127, is intended to transpose Directive (EU) 2023/970 of the European Parliament and of the Council on pay transparency into Polish law. According to information published by the RCL, the draft remains open, and its documentation was last updated on 4 May 2026.

The new regulations do not entail simple, blanket ‘salary transparency’ for every employee. Their essence is different: employers are to establish more transparent, objective and gender-neutral rules for determining pay. The aim of the regulation is to reduce unjustified pay gaps between women and men and to make it easier for employees to assert their right to equal pay for equal work or work of equal value. The explanatory memorandum to the draft states that the lack of transparency in pay systems currently makes it difficult to identify and challenge pay discrimination.

## Employers’ obligations

The draft primarily provides for an obligation to streamline the pay structure. Employers will have to base pay decisions on objective criteria, such as qualifications, skills, effort, responsibility or working conditions. It will also be essential to correctly define categories of employees performing work of equal value or work of equal worth. In practice, this may require a review of pay scales, remuneration policies, bonus schemes, job descriptions and HR documentation.

## Employees’ rights

One of the most important rights for employees will be the right to information regarding their individual pay level and average pay levels for categories of employees performing work of equal value or of equal worth — broken down by gender. The draft also sets out tasks for the equality body, which will be able to request such information from the employer at the employee’s request.

## Pay gap reporting

An important part of the new regulations will be pay gap reporting. The monitoring body is, among other things, to provide employers with an IT tool for preparing reports, collect information from a joint pay assessment, and publish specific data. The draft also provides for broader powers for the National Labour Inspectorate, including the ability to request information regarding the pay gap and to participate in remedial measures.

## Response mechanism and sanctions

The mechanism for responding to unjustified pay differences will be particularly important for employers. If a pay analysis reveals a gap that cannot be justified by objective and gender-neutral criteria, it may be necessary to implement remedial measures and, in certain cases, to carry out a so-called joint pay assessment involving employee representatives. This means that pay will no longer be solely a matter of internal company policy — it will become an area requiring documentation, measurement and justification. The draft also provides for sanctions. These are intended to be proportionate yet dissuasive, meaning that failure to implement the relevant procedures, a lack of required information, or incorrect reporting may entail a real risk of inspection and financial penalties.

## Where to start?

It is worth noting that the draft has not yet been passed into law. However, this does not alter the fact that employers should start preparing now. The most sensible first step is to audit the remuneration policy: reviewing regulations, pay scales, bonus schemes, job descriptions, HR and payroll data, and procedures for providing information to employees. The larger the organisation, the greater the importance of data quality and the consistency of justifications for pay differences.
