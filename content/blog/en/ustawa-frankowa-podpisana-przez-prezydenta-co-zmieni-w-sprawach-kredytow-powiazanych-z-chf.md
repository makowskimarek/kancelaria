## The Act has been signed; we are awaiting its publication

On 17 July 2026, the President signed the Act of 29 May 2026 on special measures concerning the examination of cases relating to credit agreements denominated in or indexed to the Swiss franc concluded with consumers.

At the time of writing, the Act has not yet been published in the Journal of Laws. However, this is essentially the final stage of the legislative process. Following publication, it will be necessary to determine the exact date on which the individual provisions come into force and their impact on cases already pending before the courts.

It is worth emphasising that the Act does not resolve the substance of Swiss franc disputes. It does not automatically declare a given contract to be invalid, does not replace an analysis of a specific contract, and does not eliminate the need to formulate claims correctly. Its main aim is to streamline the conduct of court proceedings concerning loans denominated in or indexed to the CHF.

## Why were specific provisions introduced for Swiss franc cases?

The draft Act was a response to the scale of the Swiss franc disputes and the resulting burden on the courts. The government’s explanatory memorandum to the draft explicitly stated that the aim of the legislation is to establish standards enabling the courts to hear cases concerning loans denominated in or indexed to the Swiss franc more swiftly, whilst remaining consistent with the case law of the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) and the Supreme Court.

The legislator highlighted, amongst other things, the need to alleviate the backlog of court cases, protect citizens’ right to have their cases heard within a reasonable time, streamline the work of the courts, and encourage the parties to settle disputes amicably.

From the consumers’ point of view, the most important aspect is that the new provisions are intended to limit situations where a Swiss franc case drags on for many years, and the borrower remains in a state of uncertainty throughout this period regarding the validity of the contract, future settlements with the bank and the risks associated with settling with the bank.

## Automatic suspension of instalment payments upon service of the claim on the bank

One of the most important measures provided for in the Act is the suspension of the consumer’s obligation to fulfil their obligations under the Swiss franc loan agreement upon service of the claim on the bank. According to the draft’s rationale, this measure was intended to reduce the need to file and process separate applications for interim relief.

For borrowers, this could be a significant practical change. Until now, in many cases it was necessary to file an application for interim relief and wait for the court’s decision, which, in our experience, is predictable, but unfortunately still not certain, despite clear case law from the Supreme Court, and deviations from the established line of case law continue to occur. Once the new provisions come into force, the protective mechanism will derive directly from the Act, although its application in a specific case will still require careful procedural analysis.

This does not mean that every borrower should automatically cease payments without consulting a solicitor. Suspending repayments, settling payments made to date, the risk of set-off, any claims by the bank and the wording of the statement of claim should be assessed on a case-by-case basis, in the context of the borrower’s factual circumstances and the terms of the agreements binding them to the bank.

## More cases may be resolved more quickly

The Act also provides for procedural measures designed to speed up the hearing of cases. These include, amongst others, a wider scope for ruling in closed session, the examination of the parties in writing, facilitations regarding the examination of witnesses outside the courtroom, restrictions on the principle of collegial adjudication, and the granting of specific powers to court clerks in cases relating to the withdrawal of a claim or appeal.

These are technical changes, but their practical significance may be considerable. In cases involving Swiss franc loans, many circumstances recur in hundreds or thousands of proceedings, and the fundamental legal issues have already been assessed on numerous occasions by the CJEU, the Supreme Court and the ordinary courts at virtually all levels. Shortening the evidence-taking stage and granting the court greater flexibility may therefore realistically lead to a swifter resolution of the case.

It must be borne in mind, however, that a Swiss franc case remains a standard legal dispute. The content of the contract, the manner of its performance, the repayment history, any amendments, previous settlements, consumer status and the correct settlement of mutual obligations are still of key importance.

## A single set of proceedings instead of several disputes over settlements

The draft also provided for mechanisms to settle the bank’s and the consumer’s counterclaims in a single set of proceedings. This includes, amongst other things, the possibility of filing an application for the settlement of a counterclaim and clarifying the rules on set-off.

This is important because, following a declaration of the contract’s invalidity, a further stage of the dispute would often arise: how much the parties should reimburse each other, whether the bank can effectively set off its claim, from when interest is due, and whether it is necessary to conduct separate proceedings covering the bank’s claims. The new provisions are intended to ensure that the settlement process is more focused and less fragmented in procedural terms. Our experience also shows that, in disputes concerning the invalidity of a contract, banks adopt positions that are diametrically opposed to those they express in proceedings concerning the use of capital. Once these changes come into force, this discrepancy should disappear, simplifying the proceedings.

For the consumer, this may mean greater clarity regarding the final outcome of the case. Instead of a judgment addressing only part of the issue, it will be possible to reach a practical settlement between the parties more efficiently.

## Enforceability of first-instance judgments

One of the measures strengthening the consumer’s position is to make a first-instance judgment awarding a monetary payment to the consumer enforceable as soon as it is announced or served on the bank, even if the judgment was delivered in closed session. This solution was identified in the government’s explanatory memorandum as a measure to safeguard consumers’ interests.

In practice, this may reduce the bank’s institutional advantage arising from the long wait for a final and binding judgment. Of course, each case requires an assessment of the risks involved, including those associated with further appeal proceedings. This does not, however, alter the fact that the legislator has clearly recognised the need to strengthen the effectiveness of consumer protection in Swiss franc disputes.

## What about loans in euros, US dollars or other currencies?

It is very important to clarify the scope of the Act. The new provisions apply to cases involving loan agreements concluded with consumers that are denominated in or indexed to the Swiss franc. Despite calls from consumer organisations, the new provisions do not cover loans denominated in or indexed to other currencies, such as the euro, the US dollar, the yen or other foreign currencies.

This does not, however, mean that borrowers with such agreements are left without protection. The mechanisms used in foreign-currency agreements were often similar: reference to the bank’s exchange rate tables, a lack of transparent rules for setting exchange rates, currency spreads, information asymmetry, exchange rate risk passed on to the consumer, and unclear provisions regarding conversions.

Therefore, agreements linked to other currencies are also worth analysing. They will not benefit from the special procedure provided for in the Swiss franc Act, but may be challenged on the basis of civil law, consumer law and case law concerning prohibited contractual terms. In such cases, it is crucial to examine in detail the structure of the contract, the method of loan disbursement, the repayment terms, the conversion mechanism and the information provided to the consumer prior to concluding the contract.

## Experience matters

Disputes concerning Swiss franc loans are no longer an experimental area. Case law is much more established than it was a few years ago, but this does not mean that every case is straightforward. Banks continue to raise defences relating to set-off, retention, limitation periods, the settlement of principal and interest, consumer status, and the effects of previous amendments and settlements.

The new Act may streamline the handling of cases, but it cannot replace litigation experience. A well-prepared claim, the correct formulation of claims, accurate calculations and the ability to anticipate the bank’s arguments will continue to be of paramount importance.

## What should you do now?

The President’s signing of the Act is a good time to take another look at your loan agreement. This applies both to those who have not yet taken legal action against the bank and to those whose cases are already pending. Once the Act has been published in the Journal of Laws, it will be necessary to assess which of the new provisions will apply to specific proceedings and how they may affect the future litigation strategy.

In the case of Swiss franc loans, it will be important to determine whether the new provisions may speed up the case, facilitate the suspension of payments, assist in the settlement of claims, or alter the conduct of the evidential proceedings. In the case of loans linked to other currencies, however, it is necessary to check whether the structure of the agreement allows claims to be made under general principles.
