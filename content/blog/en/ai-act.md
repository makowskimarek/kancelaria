## The AI Act in the food sector — new obligations at the intersection of artificial intelligence and food law

The AI Act is what is known as a ‘horizontal regulation’. This means that it does not apply solely to software producers or large technology platforms, but its provisions will also be relevant to businesses that use artificial intelligence in their day-to-day operations — including food manufacturers, distributors, retail chains, importers, laboratories, e-commerce operators and supply chain service providers.

In the food sector, artificial intelligence can be used, amongst other things, for sales forecasting, quality control, analysing complaints, monitoring the cold chain, stock management, supplier assessment, creating product descriptions, personalising offers and customer service. Every day, we discover new applications for it.

However, not every such application will involve the same level of risk. A tool supporting the preparation of a marketing campaign or the graphics used in product branding should be assessed differently from a system that recommends whether to release a batch of products for sale or classifies the risk associated with a raw material supplier.

## When does the AI Act come into force?

The AI Act came into force on 1 August 2024, but its implementation has been phased in. From 2 February 2025, the general provisions apply, including obligations regarding AI competence and prohibitions on certain AI practices. From 2 August 2025, regulations concerning, amongst other things, general-purpose AI models and the EU AI governance system came into force. As a rule, these did not introduce any significant changes to the day-to-day operations of AI beneficiaries. Most of the provisions of the AI Act apply from 2 August 2026, including those affecting the labelling of AI use in product design, whilst the full implementation of the key milestones has been phased in until 2 August 2028.

For food businesses, this means that preparations for the AI Act should not be left until the last minute. The first step is to determine whether the organisation uses AI systems at all, in which processes they are used, and whether their outputs could affect food safety, product compliance, quality documentation, advertising or consumer relations.

## AI and responsibility for food safety

From a food law perspective, in accordance with Regulation 178/2002, the fundamental principle remains unchanged: it is the business operator active in the food market who is responsible for ensuring the product’s compliance with the law.

AI can support the fulfilment of these obligations, but it does not replace the business operator’s responsibility. If an AI system incorrectly assesses a risk, classifies a batch as compliant, fails to detect irregularities in the cold chain, or generates incorrect product information, regulatory and commercial liability will still rest with the operator placing the product on the market.

Therefore, in the food industry, human oversight of the results produced by AI systems is of particular importance. Automation can streamline processes, but it should not lead to a situation where decisions critical to food safety or labelling compliance are made without genuine substantive control.

## Areas of particular risk in the food industry

The first area requiring attention is quality control. Vision systems, algorithms assessing batch parameters, tools analysing laboratory results, or models predicting microbiological risk can be extremely useful. At the same time, business operators should know what data the system is based on, how its results are documented, and who approves the decisions made on that basis.

The second area is traceability. Food law requires that food be traceable at all stages of production, processing and distribution. In practice, this means there is an obligation to have procedures in place to determine the source of raw materials or products and to whom they were subsequently supplied. AI can greatly facilitate the analysis of large datasets, the identification of at-risk batches or the management of product recalls, but the outcome of the algorithm’s operation should be verifiable and documentable.

The third area is labelling and advertising. AI tools can generate product descriptions, advertising slogans, graphics, responses to consumers or content for online shops. In the food industry, however, such materials must meet stringent requirements regarding accurate food information. The risk lies in the fact that AI may create content that is marketing-friendly but unlawful – for example, suggesting health benefits of a product that cannot be claimed, or omitting significant restrictions. Furthermore, any use of AI-generated deepfake content should be disclosed, which refers to images, recordings or videos generated or modified by AI that resemble existing people, objects, places, entities or events, and which may be mistakenly perceived as authentic or genuine.

The fourth area concerns complaints and communication with consumers. A chatbot or a system for categorising complaints can speed up the handling process, but if a complaint concerns product safety, allergens, possible contamination or an adverse reaction following consumption, the organisation should have a clear escalation procedure to the person responsible for quality, food safety or compliance.

## What should a business do in practice?

The first step should be to carry out an inventory of AI systems. The business should determine which tools it uses, who the suppliers are, in which processes they are used, what data they process, and whether their results influence decisions of regulatory significance.

The second step is risk classification. Not every use of AI in a food business will require the same procedures. A tool for organising marketing notes will carry a different level of risk than a system used for analysing raw material quality, managing allergens, approving labels or handling food safety incidents.

The third step is to implement oversight policies. The business should specify which AI outputs require human approval, how decisions are documented, when to deviate from the system’s recommendations, and who is responsible for the final assessment of product compliance.

The fourth step is to review contracts with technology suppliers. It is worth checking whether the contract specifies the supplier’s role, scope of responsibility, rules for system updates, access to documentation, the procedure for reporting errors, data security and support in the event of an audit or incident.

The fifth step is to train those using AI. The AI Act emphasises the importance of AI-related competencies, and in the food industry these competencies should be understood in a broader sense than merely technical. Employees should know when they can use an AI tool, when legal or quality verification is required, and what data should not be entered into the system.

These obligations are important not only from the perspective of implementing the AI Act, but also from the perspective of trade secrets or the protection of personal data – often, when using AI models, we do not even know who our data is being sent to or how it is being processed. This requires due diligence on the part of every employee using AI to prevent data deemed confidential from leaving the organisation.

## The AI Act as part of food compliance

The AI Act does not replace food law. However, it overlaps with obligations that food business operators are already familiar with: product safety, traceability, accurate consumer information, documentation of decisions, supplier management and incident response.

For this reason, the implementation of the AI Act should not be treated solely as a project confined to a single area. In a food business, it should be a project involving the legal, quality assurance, production, marketing, procurement, sales and technology departments – and the findings of which should be communicated to all staff, as we ourselves may often be unaware of how they are making their lives easier by using AI. Only such an approach allows us to assess whether artificial intelligence actually supports legal compliance or whether it creates new risks that have not yet been addressed in procedures.
