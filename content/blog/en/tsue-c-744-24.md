Positive implications for consumers with consumer credit

The judgment of the Court of Justice of the European Union of 23 April 2026 in Case C-744/24 may be of significant importance to consumers who have entered into consumer credit agreements, including cash loans, instalment loans or other financing agreements covered by the Consumer Credit Act. The case concerned the practice whereby a bank charged interest not only on the amount actually paid out to the consumer, but also on amounts intended to cover costs associated with the loan, such as insurance premiums. The Court held that such a practice is incompatible with Directive 2008/48/EC on consumer credit agreements.

The significance of this ruling extends beyond the individual dispute involving a single bank. The CJEU recalled that the ‘total amount of the credit’ and the ‘total cost of the credit to the consumer’ are distinct and mutually exclusive concepts. In other words, the total amount of the credit cannot include costs which the consumer is obliged to bear in connection with the conclusion or performance of the contract, such as fees, commissions or insurance costs. Interest should be calculated on the amount actually made available to the consumer, and not on the costs of the credit financed by the lender.

In practice, this means that consumers are now in a better position to challenge contracts in which a bank or lending institution has charged interest on the financed costs of the credit. This applies in particular to situations where the agreement specified a loan amount comprising not only the funds paid out to the customer, but also a commission, an insurance premium or other charges, and interest was then calculated on this increased amount. This type of arrangement could have led to an increase in the total cost of the loan, even though the consumer did not have the full amount on which interest was charged at their free disposal.

## ‘Free loan’ penalty

Judgment C-744/24 is particularly significant in cases concerning the so-called ‘free loan’ penalty, as it confirms the Court’s existing pro-consumer line of case law. The judgment confirms that charging interest on the costs financed by the loan infringes the provisions of the Consumer Credit Act, which transposes Directive 2008/48/EC, and that such an infringement may be subject to the sanctions provided for in that Act, including the ‘free loan’ sanction.

The ‘free loan’ sanction essentially means that the consumer repays the lender only the principal, without interest or other loan costs. However, this does not mean that every consumer credit agreement afflicted with a similar defect automatically becomes ‘free’ following the CJEU’s judgment. Each agreement requires individual analysis. It is necessary to examine its content, the method of calculating the total loan amount, the total cost of the loan, the APR, the repayment schedule, and whether the lender has correctly fulfilled its information obligations towards the consumer.

## Implications for national case law

It is worth emphasising that the CJEU does not directly rule on a specific national case. Under the preliminary ruling procedure, the Court interprets EU law, and it is then for the national court to resolve the dispute in accordance with that interpretation. At the same time, the CJEU’s judgment is also binding on other national courts that will be dealing with similar legal issues. In practice, this may lead to greater consistency in case law in cases where consumers challenge the method of calculating interest on credit costs. We have seen this mechanism operate effectively in the so-called ‘Swiss franc cases’, where the CJEU’s ruling led to the harmonisation of Polish case-law and, ultimately, a significant improvement in the legal position of consumers.

A possible beneficial consequence for consumers is therefore not only the possibility of claiming a refund of part of the unduly charged amounts, but also the strengthening of their case in legal disputes with banks and lending institutions. The judgement may be relevant both to those who are already involved in a dispute and to those who are only just analysing their contract. This applies in particular to loans where commissions, insurance or other costs have been ‘added’ to the loan amount and subsequently subject to interest.

## What to check in your contract

However, the implications of the ruling should not be oversimplified. The mere fact of having entered into a consumer credit agreement is not sufficient to successfully pursue a claim. Factors of significance include, amongst others, the date the agreement was concluded, its type, the borrower’s status as a consumer, the loan amount, the content of the information form, the manner in which costs are presented, and whether the claim is time-barred. In many cases, it will also be crucial to correctly word the declaration invoking the ‘free loan’ remedy and to prepare arguments in the event of a refusal by the lender.

From a legal perspective, the judgment in Case C-744/24 forms part of a broader line of CJEU case law concerning consumer protection in the financial market. The Court consistently emphasises the importance of contractual transparency, clearly informing consumers about the costs of financing, and the ability to compare loan offers. In the judgment in question, the CJEU pointed out that transparency in the consumer credit market, including accurate information on the APR, serves to enable consumers to compare offers more easily and make an informed decision.

For consumers, the practical conclusion is simple: it is worth reviewing consumer credit agreements, particularly those involving commissions, insurance or other non-interest-bearing costs of the loan. The analysis should cover not only the amount of the costs themselves, but also how they are set out in the agreement and the repayment schedule. In many cases, only a detailed calculation of the agreement allows one to assess whether the lender charged interest on amounts that were not actually paid out to the consumer.
