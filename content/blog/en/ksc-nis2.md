The new regulations on the National Cybersecurity System pose a significant challenge for businesses designated as important or critical entities. What is the timetable for the new obligations coming into force?

In the case of the amendment to the Act on the National Cybersecurity System, a significant change should be noted: we are no longer talking solely about a draft. The Act of 23 January 2026 amending the Act on the National Cybersecurity System and certain other acts was published in the Journal of Laws on 2 March 2026, and, in accordance with Article 49 thereof, it entered into force one month after the date of publication, i.e. on 3 April 2026.

The amendment transposes the NIS2 Directive into Polish law and significantly expands the scope of entities subject to cybersecurity obligations. The previous model, based, among other things, on operators of essential services, is replaced by a broader system covering critical entities and important entities. For businesses, this means they must carry out self-identification, i.e. determine whether their activities fall within the sectors, sub-sectors and categories specified in the Act and its annexes. The Ministry of Digital Affairs explicitly states that the self-identification process is carried out independently by the entity concerned.

## Implementation timetable

The first practical deadline in the timetable was 13 April 2026. — from this date, the Minister for Digital Affairs began ex officio entries into the KSC Register of existing operators of key services, trust service providers, telecommunications operators and public entities. However, not all entities will be entered automatically. From 7 May 2026, the option of self-registration in the KSC Register was launched for entities not covered by ex officio entry.

The key deadline for entities which, on the date the Act came into force, met the criteria for being classified as a key or important entity, is the deadline for submitting an application for entry in the register. The Act itself refers in this regard to the timetable announced by the minister responsible for digitalisation, and the Ministry of Digital Affairs publishes the key dates for entities covered by the new regulations. As a general rule, a key or important entity must submit an application for inclusion in the register within 6 months of the date on which it meets the criteria for being classified as such.

The next key stage is the implementation of organisational and technical obligations. Entities which, on the date the Act came into force, met the criteria for being recognised as a key or important entity, have 12 months from the date the Act came into force to fulfil the obligations set out in Chapter 3 of the Act. In practice, this means a deadline of 3 April 2027 for the full implementation of, among other things, an information security management system, risk management policies, incident response procedures, business continuity measures, cyber hygiene, access control, asset management and supply chain security.

The amendment also provides for a separate audit deadline. Key entities that met the criteria for coverage under the Act on the date of its entry into force are required to conduct the first mandatory audit within 24 months of the Act’s entry into force, i.e. in principle by 3 April 2028.

## Financial penalties — an important caveat

It is also worth noting the deadline regarding financial penalties. The Act provides that certain financial penalties may only be imposed for the first time two years after the Act comes into force. However, this should not be treated as a two-year exemption from obligations. The deadlines for registration, implementation of an information security management system and fulfilment of other obligations run independently of the deferred imposition of certain sanctions.

## Practical preparation schedule

For businesses, a practical preparation schedule should look as follows: first, self-identification and determining whether the organisation is a key or important entity; then, entry into the KSC Register or verification of an entry made ex officio; subsequently, an audit of gaps against the new requirements; followed by the implementation of documentation, procedures and technical measures; and finally, preparing the organisation for an audit and inspection by the competent authority.

The biggest mistake would be to reduce the amendment to the KSC solely to the formal obligation of entry in the register. Entry is only the beginning of the process. What will really matter for a business are the procedures for risk management, incident handling, business continuity, ICT supplier security, documenting management decisions, and demonstrating that the measures taken are proportionate to the scale of operations and risks.
